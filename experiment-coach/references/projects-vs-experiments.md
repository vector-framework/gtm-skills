# Projects vs. Experiments: Two Types of Work

## The Fundamental Separation

Not all objectives are the same. Some you know how to achieve. Some you need to figure out. Treating them the same way is one of the fastest paths to missed goals.

**Project work** — You know what to do and are confident the plan will deliver the outcome. Confidence threshold is >80%. You plan initiatives upfront and execute.

**Experiment work** — You don't know what will work. There's a gap between what you want and what you understand about how to get there. The focus is on closing the knowledge gap as fast as possible.

## How to tell which is which

If you can plan the initiatives with high confidence that they'll deliver the key results, it's a Project. If you're essentially saying "we think this might work but we need to find out," it's an Experiment.

The telltale sign of a misclassified Experiment: it has numeric targets ("increase revenue by 20%") instead of learning objectives ("find out whether X approach works"). If you knew enough to set a confident numeric target, it would be in Project, not Experiment.

## How Experiment OKRs work

- Start with the delta between what Project OKRs will deliver and what the Golden Goal requires
- Frame objectives as learning questions: "Find out how to..." not "Increase..."
- Start broad, narrow as learning accumulates
- Review and update weekly at the meta meeting
- Measure by whether you closed a knowledge gap, not whether you hit a number

Good experiment measures: "We now know that X is true." "We've validated that Y works at small scale." "We've ruled out Z as a viable channel."

Bad experiment measures: "We increased revenue by W%." That's a Project measure applied to Experiment work.

## The cultural signal

Framing experiment objectives as questions communicates to the whole team that you've got a different hat on. You're not executing a plan. You're trying to figure out the answer. The knowledge game is the success — when you know all the answers, the doing is easy.
