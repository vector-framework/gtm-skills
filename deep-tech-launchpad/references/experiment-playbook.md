# Stage 5 growth experiment playbook

By Stage 5 the operator has 1–2 hypotheses with real evidence behind them. The purpose of this stage is to design experiments that generate commercial signal fast, with clean kill criteria so failing experiments don't quietly linger.

## Principles

**1. Direct signal over proxy signal.** A buyer agreeing to a 30-day paid pilot is direct signal. A buyer downloading a whitepaper is proxy signal. Early-stage B2B dies on proxy metrics. Design for direct.

**2. Timebox everything.** A 6-week experiment has energy. A 6-month experiment becomes the roadmap by accident. The clock is your friend.

**3. Pre-commit to the kill criterion.** Write down, before starting, the result at which you'd stop. Without it, sunk cost wins every time.

**4. Learning capture is the deliverable.** Even a "successful" experiment is worthless if you didn't capture *why* it worked. A failed experiment with strong artefacts (quotes, objections, decision timelines) is better than a vague success.

**5. One variable at a time.** If you change the segment, the channel, *and* the offer shape, you won't know which changed the outcome. Pick one lever per experiment.

## Experiment patterns for early-stage B2B

### Pattern A — Narrow cold outbound

**When to use**: you've picked a segment, you have a list of ~50 named companies, and you want to test whether the messaging pulls. The fastest way to generate demand-side signal without pre-existing warm pipeline.

**Shape**: 50 named target accounts × 2–3 personas per account × personalised outreach referencing the triggering situation identified in research. No generic pitches. No templates.

**Success threshold**: target 3–5% meeting-conversion rate at this level of personalisation, with meetings yielding qualifying language ("we're dealing with exactly this", "who else have you spoken to?").

**Kill criterion**: fewer than 3 qualified meetings from the first 50, *or* meetings where the target does not recognise the pain in their own words. The latter is often more important than the former.

### Pattern B — Paid pilot conversion

**When to use**: you have active conversations with prospects who are polite but haven't moved. The purpose is to force the commercial question.

**Shape**: take 3–5 prospects from current pipeline and offer a 30–60 day paid pilot with a specific success metric and a clear conversion path. Paid is the point — free pilots in B2B are notorious for never converting.

**Success threshold**: 2 of 5 convert to pilot, 1 of those converts to paid annual at pilot end, citing the success metric.

**Kill criterion**: zero prospects willing to sign paid pilot, even at modest price. This is a signal the pull isn't real.

### Pattern C — Concentrated community play

**When to use**: your segment is a tight community (e.g., YC founders, defence primes, FinOps leaders) where reputation compounds fast.

**Shape**: identify the 1–2 highest-leverage community moments in the next quarter (a conference, a podcast, a Slack community, a specific LinkedIn thread leader). Show up with a sharp demand-side statement (not a pitch). Offer to work with 2–3 design-partner customers from the community.

**Success threshold**: 2 design-partner LOIs within 6 weeks, from companies the operator didn't already know.

**Kill criterion**: no inbound signal after the community touchpoint. In tight communities, silence is the answer.

### Pattern D — Technical POC bakeoff

**When to use**: deep tech with a crisp technical advantage, targeting buyers who run evaluations as standard practice (security, ML infra, compliance).

**Shape**: identify 2–3 target accounts running (or about to run) a formal POC. Offer to enter the bakeoff. Invest in winning it cleanly, documenting the result, and turning the win into a reference.

**Success threshold**: win 2 of 3 bakeoffs with clean numerical results; at least one customer agrees to reference.

**Kill criterion**: cannot even enter the bakeoff (gatekept), *or* enters and loses on a dimension that the architecture can't fix.

### Pattern E — Targeted content wedge

**When to use**: you need to build surface area with a segment without the reach to cold-outbound at scale. Slower than other patterns.

**Shape**: write 3–5 pieces of content that name the specific pain of the segment, in their own language (use verbatim quotes from Stage 4 research). Distribute through the channels the segment actually reads (often: specific newsletters, Slack communities, LinkedIn of named practitioners — *not* the company blog).

**Success threshold**: 10+ inbound conversations within 8 weeks from people who name the pain in their first message.

**Kill criterion**: content lands, traffic grows, but zero sales conversations emerge. The wedge misses.

## The experiment one-pager

Close Stage 5 with a one-pager per experiment. Format:

**Hypothesis being tested**: \[the Stage 3 sentence, unchanged\]

**Segment**: \[specific role + situation + pain\]

**Channel and mechanism**: \[the pattern chosen, plus specifics\]

**Offer shape**: \[what the buyer gets and at what commitment level\]

**Success threshold**: \[observable leading indicator within weeks\]

**Kill criterion**: \[the result that would stop investment\]

**Learning capture**: \[what will be recorded — quotes, objections, timelines, conversion rate — and where it will live\]

**Duration**: \[weeks\]

**Review date**: \[specific date\]

**Owner**: \[named person\]

Anything less than this is a wish.

## Anti-patterns

**The "always-on" experiment** — outbound that runs forever at steady low conversion. Kill it or double down. Limbo kills companies.

**The indefinite pilot** — a free pilot that never converts because the conversion path was never defined. Always define the paid step up-front.

**The board-slide experiment** — an experiment chosen because it looks good in a deck rather than because it produces learning. Easily spotted: if the success threshold is a vanity metric, it's a board-slide experiment.

**The pivot disguised as an experiment** — if the "experiment" is really a change in what the company does, name it as a pivot and evaluate it as such, with its own Stage 1–3 pass.
