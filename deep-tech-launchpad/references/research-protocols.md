# Stage 4 research protocols

Research exists to close specific Unknowns from the Stage 2 ledger. Always tie each research activity back to a named gap — if you can't name which open question a piece of research closes, drop it.

## The four research modes

### Mode 1 — Desk research

Goal: understand what's publicly observable about the segment without yet talking to anyone.

Sources worth mining, ranked by signal:

1. **Job postings** — the clearest public signal of what a company is paying to solve. Look at the last 3–6 months of postings in the target segment. What titles are growing? What skills are requested? What tools do they namecheck?
2. **Regulatory and compliance filings** — in regulated segments (med device, defence, finance, energy), filings reveal forced timelines and budget drivers that founders rarely understand from the outside.
3. **Analyst reports and market sizing** — useful for scale, mostly useless for timing or segmentation. Read critically.
4. **Conference talks and podcasts** — what are the segment's own leaders publicly worrying about? This is often closer to live pain than press releases.
5. **Funding data** — which companies in the adjacent space have raised recently? What did they pitch? (This is competitive intelligence and timing intelligence in one.)
6. **LinkedIn mapping of the target role** — build a list of 30–50 real people in the target role, across the target segment. You'll use this in Modes 2 and 4.

What to extract: trigger events, timeline pressure, current tooling, organisational structure, budget ownership.

### Mode 2 — Demand-side switching interviews

Goal: understand why (and whether) people in the segment have switched to anything adjacent recently. This is the Bob Moesta / Chris Spiek protocol.

Per interview:

- Target: 5–10 people in the named segment (role + situation).
- Method: 45–60 minute timeline-reconstruction interviews. Start with "let's walk back to the moment you first thought 'we need to do something about this'" and work forward through the decision. Ask about each force: what pushed them to act? What pulled them toward the option they chose? What worries slowed them? What habits or sunk costs held them in place?
- Output per interview: a timeline, the four forces mapped, the stakeholders involved, the language the interviewee uses (capture verbatim phrases for later copy work).

What you're looking for:

- **Common triggers** — if three or more interviewees describe the same triggering event, you've found a campaignable moment.
- **Common struggle language** — the words real buyers use for the pain are almost never the words founders use on their website.
- **Anxiety patterns** — what makes switching *scary* in this segment? (Regulation? Political risk? Vendor concentration?)
- **Habit patterns** — what's been "good enough" for years, and why?
- **Decision shape** — who decides, who champions, who blocks. In B2B this is often 3–5 people, not one.

**Do not** pitch during switching interviews. Do not describe your product. The interview is corrupted the moment it becomes a demo.

If the operator only has capacity for 3–5 interviews in this round, that's fine — name the sample size and don't over-claim the findings.

### Mode 3 — Alternatives and status-quo mapping

Goal: map what the segment is doing *today* in lieu of your product. The status quo is your real competitor.

For each interview or desk source, extract:

- What tools/vendors/processes are currently used?
- What's the workaround or manual hack filling the gap?
- What's the cost of the status quo (time, errors, churn, compliance risk)?
- Why hasn't the status quo been replaced yet? (This is the most important question. The answer usually reveals the anxiety or habit force.)

Build a simple table: current approach → cost → reason it persists. This table is the single best input to your positioning: you are not selling "better", you are selling "the moment the status quo finally breaks".

### Mode 4 — Pricing and procurement reconnaissance

Goal: understand how this segment *buys*, not just what they'd say about your product.

Questions to answer before Stage 5:

- What's the typical contract shape? (Annual subscription? Usage-based? Professional services? Enterprise license?)
- What's the approval threshold below which a department head can self-serve, and above which procurement and legal must get involved?
- What's the budget cycle? Who holds the budget? What fiscal year are they on?
- What's the typical time from first meeting to signed contract? Six weeks? Nine months?
- Are there preferred-vendor, security-review, or compliance-gate requirements that act as hidden barriers?

Sources: the interviewees from Mode 2 will often volunteer this if you ask at the end of the conversation. Lawyers, procurement consultants, and recent hires from the segment (they remember buying experiences vividly) are also strong sources.

## Designing the research pass

For each prioritised hypothesis, draft a short research plan:

- The 3–5 Unknowns from the Stage 2 ledger this pass will close
- Which modes (1–4) will be used, and which Unknown each targets
- Target number of interviews, target companies, target roles
- What a *positive* result looks like (the evidence pattern that would *support* the hypothesis)
- What a *negative* result looks like (the evidence pattern that would *kill* it)
- A kill criterion — if the negative pattern appears, what would be a clean-enough kill to stop investing in this hypothesis?

## Avoiding confirmation bias

Early-stage founders, even honest ones, unconsciously interview to confirm. Counter-patterns:

- Use interviewees the operator didn't sell to personally. Warmer cohorts flatter.
- Write interview questions *before* conducting — resist improvisation that steers toward confirmation.
- When synthesising, pair every "supports hypothesis" bullet with an "alternative explanation" bullet.
- If literally every interview supports the hypothesis, be suspicious. Either the hypothesis is trivially true, or the sample is biased.
