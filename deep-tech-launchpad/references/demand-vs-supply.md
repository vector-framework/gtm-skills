# Demand-side vs supply-side

This is the single most common conceptual slip in early-stage B2B and deep tech GTM. Founders describe their product in terms of what it *is* (supply-side) rather than what progress the buyer is trying to make (demand-side). Your job is to catch the slip every time and flip the frame.

## Quick test

Read a statement about the product or market. Ask: *could the buyer have said this about themselves, in their own words, describing their own life?*

If the answer is no, it's supply-side.

## Common supply-side framings and their demand-side flips

| Supply-side (what the product is) | Demand-side flip (what progress the buyer is trying to make) |
| --- | --- |
| "Runs on-device rather than cloud" | "Lets the buyer ship a connected product without convincing their CISO to trust another cloud vendor" |
| "Uses post-quantum cryptography" | "Removes the board-level risk that a product shipped in 2026 is insecure by 2030" |
| "Sub-millisecond inference" | "Makes the buyer's real-time user experience actually feel real-time, closing complaints they've been getting for 18 months" |
| "Integrated PKI" | "Eliminates the six weeks of engineering effort a first connected SKU usually burns on security plumbing" |
| "API-first" | "Lets the buyer's engineers ship in a sprint rather than scoping a 6-month integration project" |
| "No code required" | "Means the buyer's ops team can change the workflow without opening a ticket with engineering" |

Notice the pattern: the demand-side version always references *the buyer's situation, timeline, or pain*. It is written as if the buyer had a microphone.

## The four forces as a probe

When the operator offers a supply-side statement, probe with Bob Moesta's four forces:

- **Push** — what's pushing the buyer *away from* what they have today? What's broken, annoying, risky, or newly-unacceptable?
- **Pull** — what's the vision of a better life the buyer is moving *toward*?
- **Anxiety** — what fears or worries are stopping them from switching, even when push and pull are high?
- **Habit** — what's the inertia of the current solution? Sunk cost, political capital, procurement pain, workflow muscle memory?

Founders systematically see push and pull clearly and are blind to anxiety and habit. If an operator tells you customers "just haven't signed yet", suspect anxiety or habit before believing the deal is imminent. Pull is rarely the bottleneck.

## The "that's interesting" trap

In early-stage B2B, customers will say "that's interesting" to almost anything plausibly relevant. This is a polite reflex, not a buying signal. A rough translation:

- "That's interesting" ≈ "Please continue."
- "That's useful" ≈ "I could imagine using this."
- "When can I get it?" ≈ Actual pull.
- "Can you come meet my security team?" ≈ Demand-side pull plus procurement pathway — the gold standard in enterprise.

Push operators to downgrade "interesting" responses in their evidence base.

## Status quo is the real competitor

In early-stage B2B, the alternative the buyer is most likely to choose is *nothing*. Not your named competitor — the existing spreadsheet, the existing vendor, the existing workflow, the existing silence. Until you understand *what the buyer is doing today* and *why doing nothing is becoming unacceptable*, you don't have a demand-side picture.

When building a hypothesis, always fill in: "Today this segment handles this by \_\_\_\_, and the reason they're becoming unwilling to keep doing that is \_\_\_\_." If either blank is hard to fill, push back on the hypothesis.
